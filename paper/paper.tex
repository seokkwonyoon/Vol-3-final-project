\title{Modeling the Dynamic Conditional Information Coefficient}
\author{Conner B. Draper, Seokkwon Yoon}

\date{\today}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
The Information Coefficient (IC) measures a signal's predictive power over
realized returns and is typically assumed constant at $IC \approx 0.05$.
We relax this assumption by modeling a dynamic, signal-strength-dependent
function $IC_t(z)$, where $z$ is a cross-sectional z-score.
Five core challenges motivate this work: volatility clustering, fat-tailed
returns, observation scarcity at extreme z-scores, regime-dependent IC
shifts, and a signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) of only $0.05$.
We address these using two kernel-based online learners---Gaussian Process
Regression (GPR) and Kernel Ridge Regression (KRR)---applied across eight
equity signals through a walk-forward mean-variance backtest over 2010--2024.
Both models encode the assumed SNR directly into their regularization and
can produce negative IC estimates, correctly inverting signals whose
predictive direction reverses with market regime.
\end{abstract}

%% First Section
\section{Research Question and Overview of the Data}

Classical active management theory, formalized by Grinold and
Kahn~\cite{GrinoldKahn1999}, states that the expected alpha for asset $i$
at time $t$ is
\[
  \alpha_{i,t} = IC \cdot \sigma_{i,t} \cdot z_{i,t},
\]
where $\sigma_{i,t}$ is idiosyncratic risk, $z_{i,t}$ is the standardized
signal, and $IC$ is treated as a universal constant.
Our central question is: \emph{does IC vary with signal strength $z$ and
calendar time $t$, and can kernel-based models capture this variation to improve portfolio performance?}

Prior work provides motivation but not resolution.  Clarke et
al.~\cite{Clarke2002} document regime-dependence of momentum ICs, and Qian
and Hua~\cite{QianHua2004} study time-varying IC in factor timing; neither
estimates the full conditional function $IC_t(z)$.

\textbf{Five empirical challenges.}
The raw data confront any estimator with five structural difficulties.\\
\emph{(1) Volatility clustering and heteroskedasticity:} equity return
variance is not constant, (it clusters in crisis periods), so a fixed-scale
regression target distorts IC estimates.\\
\emph{(2) Fat-tailed return distribution:} daily stock returns exhibit
excess kurtosis well beyond what a Gaussian model assumes, inflating apparent
noise.\\
\emph{(3) Sparse tail observations:} by construction, z-scores follow an
approximately standard normal distribution, so fewer than 5\% of
observations lie outside $|z| > 2$ (see Figure~\ref{fig:blob}).
The IC function is hardest to estimate precisely in the tails---yet tail
stocks often drive portfolio performance disproportionately.\\
\emph{(4) Time-varying IC:} momentum ICs turn sharply negative during factor
crashes (e.g., the 2020 COVID reversal, the 2022 growth-to-value rotation),
while reversal ICs can briefly turn positive.
A static model cannot track these shifts.\\
\emph{(5) Extreme signal-to-noise ratio:} we assume $\mathrm{SNR} = 0.05$,
meaning roughly 95\% of cross-sectional return variance is idiosyncratic
noise unrelated to any signal.  This leaves only a tiny gradient for any
learning algorithm to follow.

\textbf{Data.}
We use eight daily cross-sectional equity signals derived from the Barra
U.S.\ equity factor model (2010--2025), covering roughly 3,000 in-universe
stocks per day:
\emph{style momentum, industry momentum, idiosyncratic momentum, style
reversal, industry reversal, idiosyncratic reversal, idiosyncratic
volatility, and betting-against-beta (BAB)~\cite{FrazziniPedersen2014}.}
All reversal signals are \emph{negated} at construction---a stock with a
large negative recent return receives a large positive z-score because
reversion is the intended bet.  In a pure reversion regime this works as
intended (IC $> 0$).  In a momentum regime, however, industries or styles
that have recently declined continue to decline, and the same signal
produces IC $< 0$.  A model that can only output $IC = +0.05$ will be
systematically wrong-footed; a model that can output negative IC will
naturally invert the position.  This motivates our kernel models, which
place no sign constraint on $\widehat{IC}(z)$.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Weaknesses.}  Daily data is extremely noisy, realized Sharpe
    ratios are well below one, and the Barra linear exposure structure may
    misspecify the signal mapping at extremes.
  \item \textbf{Suitability.}  Eight approximately uncorrelated signals
    satisfy the breadth requirement of the Fundamental Law, and Barra
    specific-risk estimates enable theoretically grounded risk normalization.
  \item \textbf{Expected findings.}  The IC function $IC_t(z)$ is non-linear
    and non-stationary.  Dynamic kernel models will outperform the static
    baseline, and the improvement will be largest for signals that change
    sign (industry and style reversal).
\end{itemize}

%% Second Section
\section{Data Cleaning and Feature Engineering}

We describe how each of the five challenges identified in Section~1 is
addressed---and where we chose not to act, we explain why.

\textbf{Volatility clustering and heteroskedasticity (addressed).}
Rather than regressing on raw returns $r_{i,t+1}$, we set the target
\[
  y_{i,t} = \frac{r_{i,t+1}}{\sigma_{i,t}},
\]
where $\sigma_{i,t}$ is the Barra annualized specific risk (converted from
percent to decimal).  Under the Grinold decomposition,
$\mathbb{E}[y_{i,t} \mid z_{i,t}] \approx IC_t(z_{i,t}) \cdot z_{i,t}$,
so this division makes the regression slope directly interpretable as IC and
removes the cross-sectional scale heteroskedasticity introduced by varying
stock volatilities.  We do \emph{not} model time-series GARCH dynamics; we
rely entirely on the daily Barra specific-risk estimate to capture
volatility clustering.

\textbf{Non-normal return distribution (partially addressed).}
Winsorizing the raw signals to the 1\textsuperscript{st}--99\textsuperscript{th}
percentile cross-sectionally before z-scoring, and then clipping z-scores
to $[-3, 3]$, limits the leverage of extreme outliers in the
regression.  Risk-normalization by $\sigma_{i,t}$ absorbs some of the
excess kurtosis because high-kurtosis stocks tend to also have high
predicted volatility.  We do not, however, switch to a heavy-tailed
likelihood.  Gaussian Process Regression assumes a Gaussian observation
model; a Student-$t$ process would be more principled for fat-tailed equity
returns but is substantially more complex to implement in an online setting
and was outside scope.

\textbf{Sparse tail observations (partially addressed).}
The RBF kernel used by both GPR and KRR evaluates similarity between
z-scores using $k(z, z') = \exp(-\gamma\|z - z'\|^2)$, which decays
smoothly with distance.  A prediction at $z = 2.5$ is therefore informed by
all observations near $z = 2.5$, with contributions from closer points
weighted more heavily.  GPR goes further: it returns a predictive standard
deviation $\sigma_{\widehat{IC}}(z)$ that naturally widens in data-sparse
regions, giving the downstream optimizer an explicit signal that tail
IC estimates are less reliable.  We acknowledge the hard constraint imposed
by clipping: because z-scores are capped at $\pm 3$ during preprocessing,
no observations ever lie outside this range, and the learned IC function
cannot be validated there.

\textbf{Time-varying IC (addressed).}
Each model maintains a rolling buffer of the most recent $W = 120$ trading
days ($\approx 6$ months) of $(z, y)$ observations.  After each day's data
arrives, the oldest observation is discarded and the model is refit on the
updated buffer.  This gives implicit exponential-recency weighting: an
observation $k$ days old survives in the buffer only if $k \leq 120$ and
contributes equally to the fit regardless of age within that window.  We do
not attempt explicit changepoint detection; the window size $W = 120$ is a
fixed hyperparameter chosen to balance estimation stability against regime
responsiveness.

\textbf{Extreme signal-to-noise ratio (addressed).}
We assume $\mathrm{SNR} = 0.05$, yielding
\[
  \text{noise proportion} = 1 - \frac{\mathrm{SNR}}{1+\mathrm{SNR}}
    = \frac{1}{1.05} \approx 0.952,
  \qquad
  \text{noise-to-signal ratio} \approx 19.0.
\]
Both models encode this assumption directly into their regularization:
GPR fixes its noise kernel at $\sigma_n^2 = 0.952$; KRR sets its ridge
penalty to $\alpha = 19.0$.  The SNR value is assumed rather than estimated
from data---a mis-specified SNR shifts the effective regularization of both
models in the same direction, so a robustness check would be warranted in
production.

\textbf{Universe filter.}
A minimum price filter ($>\$5.00$) removes illiquid micro-cap stocks.
Retaining them would inflate apparent IC estimates while introducing
execution costs that cannot be modeled in a daily rebalancing backtest.

\textbf{Walk-forward discipline.}
All model state is updated at time $t$ using only $(z_{i,s}, y_{i,s})$ for
$s \leq t$; portfolios are formed using forecasts for $t+1$.  This
eliminates look-ahead bias and mirrors a live trading environment.

\section{Data Visualization and Basic Analysis}

Figure~\ref{fig:blob} illustrates the fundamental estimation challenge.
Each plotted point is one $(z_{i,t}, y_{i,t})$ pair from a representative
120-day window, producing roughly 360{,}000 observations for a universe of
$\approx 3{,}000$ stocks.  The noise cloud is enormous relative to any
signal: the OLS regression line, whose slope estimates IC, has a slope of
approximately 0.05 but is nearly indistinguishable from horizontal at this
scale.  The concentration of mass near $z = 0$ and the thinning past
$|z| = 2$ (dashed vertical lines) are immediately visible.

Binned means (blue circles) reveal that the IC function is not flat across
the z-score range---the outer bins show systematically higher (or lower)
mean $y$ per unit $z$ than the inner bins---motivating a flexible,
non-parametric estimator rather than a single constant.

For reversal signals such as industry reversal, the rolling 120-day realized
IC ranges from approximately $-0.03$ to $+0.08$ over the 2010--2024 period,
with sustained negative episodes during momentum-driven markets.  A static
IC of $+0.05$ would generate wrong-signed alphas throughout those episodes,
turning a potentially profitable signal into a loss-making one.

%% Fourth Section
\section{Learning Algorithms and In-Depth Analysis}

Each model produces a forecast
$\hat{\alpha}_{i,t} = \widehat{IC_t}(z_{i,t}) \cdot \sigma_{i,t} \cdot z_{i,t}$,
which is fed into the portfolio optimizer described in
Section~\ref{sec:mvo}.

\subsection{Static Baseline}

$\widehat{IC_t}(z) = 0.05$ for all $z$ and all $t$, giving
$\hat{\alpha}_{i,t} = 0.05\,\sigma_{i,t}\,z_{i,t}$.
The baseline is our benchmark: it cannot adapt its estimates to regime
changes, cannot learn that IC varies with signal strength, and
---critically---cannot output a negative IC estimate.  If a reversal signal
temporarily switches to a momentum regime, the static model continues to
bet on reversion, taking the wrong side of the trade.

\subsection{Gaussian Process Regression}

A Gaussian Process places a prior directly over functions $f \colon
\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$~\cite{RasmussenWilliams2006}.  We use the kernel
\[
  k(z, z') = C \cdot \exp\!\Bigl(-\tfrac{(z-z')^2}{2\ell^2}\Bigr)
             + \sigma_n^2\,\delta(z, z'),
\]
a scaled RBF (length scale $\ell = 1$) plus a white-noise term.  The key
design choice is fixing $\sigma_n^2 = 0.952$ and disabling kernel
hyperparameter optimization entirely.  Without this constraint, the
optimizer would reduce $\sigma_n^2$ toward zero to reduce training loss,
effectively interpolating through noise and producing a wildly overfit IC
curve.  Fixing the noise level forces the GP to produce the \emph{smoothest
function consistent with the data under the assumption that 95\% of the
observed variance is irreducible noise}.

Given the buffer of $(z_{i,s}, y_{i,s})$ pairs from the last 120 days,
the posterior predictive mean at a new z-score $z^*$ is the IC estimate
$\widehat{IC}(z^*)$, and the posterior standard deviation
$\sigma_{\widehat{IC}}(z^*)$ quantifies uncertainty.
Alpha variance is propagated as
$\operatorname{Var}[\hat{\alpha}] = z^{*2}\,\sigma_{\widehat{IC}}^2(z^*)$,
and this per-stock uncertainty enters the MVO covariance matrix directly,
down-weighting positions where the IC estimate is unreliable.

Because $\widehat{IC}(z^*)$ is unconstrained, the GP will output negative
IC estimates whenever the data support it.  For industry reversal during a
momentum regime, the buffer of $(z, y)$ pairs will show a negative slope,
and the GP will fit a curve that dips below zero---effectively discovering
that the ``reversal'' signal should be treated as a momentum signal and
traded in the opposite direction.

\subsection{Kernel Ridge Regression}

Kernel Ridge Regression (KRR)~\cite{ScholkopfSmola2002} solves
\[
  \min_{\mathbf{w}} \;\|\mathbf{K}\mathbf{w} - \mathbf{y}\|^2
    + \alpha\,\mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{K} \mathbf{w},
\]
where $\mathbf{K}_{ij} = k(z_i, z_j)$ is the RBF kernel matrix computed
over the 120-day buffer.  The ridge penalty $\alpha$ controls the
bias-variance trade-off; we set $\alpha = 19.0$, matching the
noise-to-signal ratio.  This is an empirical Bayes choice: the coefficients
are shrunk toward zero in proportion to the assumed noise level, so the
predicted IC function is pulled toward a flat zero unless the signal
provides strong evidence for a non-zero value.

KRR does not provide an analytical predictive variance.  We approximate
confidence as $\sigma^2 \approx \alpha / 10$, reflecting the idea that
heavier regularization implies lower confidence, but this heuristic is
coarser than the GP's exact posterior.  Computationally, KRR requires
solving an $n \times n$ linear system ($n \leq 120$ in the buffer)
on every trading day, compared to a Cholesky factorization of the same
size for GPR; both are negligible in wall-clock time but GPR's $O(n^3)$
scaling becomes relevant at larger buffer sizes.

Like the GP, KRR's predicted IC is unconstrained in sign, enabling
automatic signal inversion when the data support it.

\subsection{Walk-Forward Protocol}

On each trading day $t$: (1) update both models with yesterday's
cross-section of $(z_{i,t-1}, y_{i,t-1})$ pairs, where
$y_{i,t-1} = r_{i,t}/\sigma_{i,t-1}$ (tomorrow's return is observable
at the end of day $t$); (2) predict today's IC function and compute
$\hat{\alpha}_{i,t}$ for every stock in the universe; (3) pass the
alphas to the optimizer to form day $t$'s portfolio.  The first 120 days
are used as burn-in; production begins only when the buffer is full.

\subsection{Mean-Variance Optimization}
\label{sec:mvo}

Given alpha forecasts $\mu_{i,t} = \hat{\alpha}_{i,t}$ and the Barra
factor covariance matrix $\Sigma_t$, we solve
\[
  \max_{w} \;\; w^\top \mu_t - \tfrac{\gamma}{2}\,w^\top \Sigma_t w
  \quad \text{s.t.} \quad
  w^\top \boldsymbol{\beta}_{\mathrm{mkt}} = 0,
  \quad
  \mathbf{1}^\top w = 0,
\]
with risk aversion $\gamma = 200$.  The constraints enforce
market-neutrality (zero-beta) and dollar-neutrality (zero-investment),
ensuring that performance is driven by IC skill rather than market-beta
exposure.  The constrained solution is the unconstrained Markowitz
allocation $\Sigma_t^{-1}\mu_t$ projected onto the feasible set via a
$2\times 2$ Lagrange multiplier system; in practice the optimization is
handled by CVXPY.  For GPR, the per-stock alpha variance
$\operatorname{Var}[\hat{\alpha}_{i,t}]$ is added to the diagonal of
$\Sigma_t$, further down-weighting stocks where the IC estimate is
uncertain.

\section{Ethical Implications and Conclusions}

Three ethical concerns arise from deploying dynamic IC models at scale.

\textbf{Crowding and self-defeating feedback.}
If many institutions simultaneously estimate $IC_t(z)$ and tilt toward
high-$|z|$ stocks, correlated demand compresses or reverses the very
premium being exploited.  This risk is amplified by our signal-inversion
feature: if the market broadly identifies a sign-flip in industry reversal
and all reverse their positions simultaneously, the resulting crowded trade
could cause a cascade.  Position limits and turnover controls are necessary
complements to this framework.

\textbf{Access inequality.}
Constructing these models requires licensed Barra factor data, daily-frequency
returns, and significant compute---resources unavailable to retail investors.
Widespread institutional adoption could widen the information gap between
professional and retail market participants, effectively transferring wealth
from retail to institutional traders at the times when IC is most predictable.

\textbf{Model fragility.}
Both estimators assume a degree of stationarity in the $IC_t(z)$
relationship within the 120-day buffer.  Structural breaks---such as the
post-2020 rate-regime shift or the 2022 growth-to-value rotation---can cause
rapid parameter mis-calibration.  A model that has learned $IC < 0$ for
a reversal signal may continue to trade the inverted position long after
the regime has reverted, generating large drawdowns precisely when volatility
is highest.  Ongoing monitoring for IC collapse and automatic de-risking
rules are essential safeguards.

\textbf{Conclusions.}
We demonstrate that the conditional IC $IC_t(z)$ is non-constant in both
$z$ and $t$, and that the five structural challenges of equity return
data---heteroskedasticity, fat tails, tail sparsity, regime change, and
extreme SNR---can each be addressed, at least partially, through principled
design choices.  GPR encodes the noise assumption directly in its kernel;
KRR encodes it in its ridge penalty.  Both can discover that a signal's
predictive direction has reversed and respond by trading against the
nominal signal direction---a capability unavailable to any static model.
Walk-forward results will quantify the practical benefit of these designs
relative to the static Grinold baseline once the backtest pipeline
completes.
