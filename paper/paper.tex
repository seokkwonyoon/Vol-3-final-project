\title{Dynamic Information Coefficients via Online Learning}
\author{Draper Conner \and Seokkwon Yoon}

\date{April 2026}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
The Information Coefficient (IC) is the standard measure of signal quality in quantitative finance,
typically held constant at a long-run average such as $IC = 0.05$.
In practice, however, predictive power may vary both with signal magnitude and over time.
We study a conditional, time-varying IC function $IC_t(z)$, where $z$ is a standardized signal value.
Using eight Barra-derived cross-sectional signals over a 30-year U.S.\ equity universe,
we compare a static baseline against four online estimators:
a polynomial Kalman filter, a recursive basis-function expansion (RBF-RLS),
a binned Kalman filter, and Nadaraya--Watson kernel regression.
Each model generates daily alpha forecasts that are fed into a
mean--variance optimizer via the Grinold formula.
We evaluate whether the added flexibility of a dynamic IC translates into improved
out-of-sample Sharpe ratios and drawdown reduction relative to the static benchmark.
\end{abstract}

%% First Section
\section{Research Question and Overview of the Data}

\textbf{Research questions.}
(i)~Does the predictive strength of a cross-sectional signal depend on its magnitude?
(ii)~If so, can an online estimate of the conditional IC improve portfolio construction
relative to a constant-IC benchmark?

\textbf{Prior work.}
Grinold and Kahn \cite{GrinoldKahn} establish the fundamental relationship
$\alpha_i = IC \cdot \sigma_i \cdot z_i$,
where $IC$ is a scalar, $\sigma_i$ is idiosyncratic volatility, and $z_i$ is a standardized signal.
The possibility that $IC$ itself depends on $z$ has received little systematic treatment.
Fama and French \cite{FamaFrench} and Carhart \cite{Carhart} motivate momentum and value signals.
Frazzini and Pedersen \cite{FrazziniPedersen} motivate the betting-against-beta signal.

\textbf{Data.}
We use the Barra USE4 model, sourced from the Silverfund research database.
The universe spans January 1995 through December 2024, covering approximately 3{,}000
in-universe U.S.\ equities per day.
For each asset and date we observe: daily total return, idiosyncratic return (\texttt{specific\_return}),
idiosyncratic risk (\texttt{specific\_risk}), predicted beta, and Barra factor exposures.
Factor return series are provided for 16 style factors (e.g.\ MOMENTUM, VALUE, SIZE)
and 59 industry factors.

We construct eight signals from these data sources (Table~\ref{tab:signals}).
All signals are cross-sectionally z-scored to zero mean and unit variance each day,
clipped to $[-3, 3]$, producing the input $z_{i,t}$ for all downstream models.

\textbf{Data weaknesses.}
Factor exposures and returns are supplied by a single vendor (Barra),
introducing model risk from the provider's own construction choices.
The universe membership filter (\texttt{in\_universe = True}) applies survivorship bias mitigation
but does not eliminate all look-ahead issues in factor construction.
Stocks below \$5 per share are excluded, which may affect small-cap momentum dynamics.

\textbf{Hypotheses.}
We expect that (a) signals with large $|z|$ are more predictive than those near zero,
producing a nonlinear IC function; (b) the shape of this function shifts across market regimes;
and (c) at least one dynamic estimator will improve upon the static 0.05 IC.

%% Second Section
\section{Data Cleaning and Feature Engineering}

\textbf{Signal normalization.}
Raw signals are computed from rolling windows (231 days for momentum, 21 days for reversal)
over log-returns, vol-scaled by dividing by the rolling standard deviation.
A final per-date cross-sectional z-score produces $z_{i,t}$.

\textbf{Training pairs.}
Each model is trained on risk-normalised returns
\[
  y_{i,t} \;=\; \frac{r_{i,t+1}}{\sigma_{i,t}},
\]
paired with $z_{i,t}$.
Dividing by $\sigma_{i,t}$ removes the scale dependence across stocks, so all models
learn a dimensionless IC function, consistent with the Grinold formulation.
Forward returns use a strict one-day lag to prevent look-ahead.

\textbf{Walk-forward design.}
Rather than a single train/test split, we run a fully sequential walk-forward:
at each date $t$ the model is updated with the previous day's $(z_{i,t-1}, y_{i,t-1})$ pairs,
then used to predict alphas for date $t$.
A 120-day rolling lookback controls how much historical data influences non-recursive models
(e.g.\ Nadaraya--Watson).

\textbf{Alpha construction.}
Given the model's prediction $\widehat{IC}_t(z)$, Grinold's formula becomes
\[
  \alpha_{i,t} \;=\; \sigma_{i,t} \cdot \widehat{IC}_t(z_{i,t}) \cdot z_{i,t}.
\]
These alphas, together with Barra covariance matrices, are fed into a
mean--variance optimizer with zero-beta and zero-investment constraints ($\lambda = 200$).

%%Fourth Section
\section{Learning Algorithms and In-depth Analysis}

We compare five IC estimation methods, each implementing the \texttt{ICModel} interface:
\texttt{update}$(z, y)$ and \texttt{predict}$(z) \to (\hat\alpha_z, \sigma^2_z)$.

\textbf{Static IC.} $IC_t(z) \equiv 0.05$ for all $z$ and $t$.
Serves as the baseline; corresponds to the textbook Grinold formula.

\textbf{Polynomial Kalman (KP).}
State $\beta_t = [\beta_0,\beta_1,\beta_2]^\top$ with
$IC_t(z) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 z + \beta_2 z^2$.
State transition is a random walk; each day's $N \approx 3{,}000$ observations update the state
via the Kalman gain $K_t = P_{t|t-1} H_t^\top (H_t P_{t|t-1} H_t^\top + R\,I)^{-1}$.
This is the ``gold standard'' in that it provides a principled uncertainty estimate for the IC curve.

\textbf{RBF-RLS.}
Seven Gaussian basis functions $\phi_k(z) = \exp\!\bigl(-(z - c_k)^2/(2h^2)\bigr)$
with centres $c_k \in \{-3,-2,\ldots,3\}$.
Weights $w$ are updated via forgetting-factor Recursive Least Squares
($\lambda = 0.995$), giving local adaptivity: only the bases near the observed $z$
values are strongly updated.

\textbf{Binned Kalman (BK).}
Fifteen equal-width bins over $[-3,3]$, each with an independent 1-D Kalman filter
tracking $IC_k$.
Observation: $y_i / z_i \approx IC_k$ for stocks in bin $k$ with $|z_i| > 0.2$.
Non-parametric; easy to visualize as a bar chart of 15 filter states.

\textbf{Nadaraya--Watson (NW).}
Rolling 120-day buffer of $(z, y)$ pairs.
Prediction: $\widehat{f}(z_{\rm new}) = \sum_i w_i y_i / \sum_i w_i$ where
$w_i = K_{\rm spatial}(z_{\rm new} - z_i)\cdot K_{\rm temporal}(t_{\rm new} - t_i)$
with Gaussian spatial kernel (bandwidth $h_s = 0.5$) and exponential temporal decay ($\tau = 60$~days).
Purely data-driven; imposes no functional form.

%%Fifth Section
\section{Ethical Implications and Conclusions}

\textbf{Ethical implications.}
The most significant risk of any systematic equity strategy is a destructive
self-fulfilling feedback loop: if many managers adopt the same conditional IC model,
their coordinated buying and selling could amplify signal-driven price moves,
eroding the very edge being exploited and potentially destabilizing markets.
This risk is especially acute for momentum signals, which have historically experienced
sharp crowding-driven crashes \cite{Carhart}.

The Barra data used here are licensed from a commercial provider.
Licensing terms restrict distribution, and any publication of factor exposures or
reconstructed factor returns could violate those agreements.
Results derived from this data should not be shared outside the research context
without explicit review.

Finally, the constant-IC assumption ($0.05$) is often presented to clients as a
conservative and prudent baseline.
Replacing it with a dynamic model that has higher in-sample Sharpe ratios
risks overfitting: a model that appears superior on historical data may fail
out-of-sample, exposing investors to unexpected losses.
Robust out-of-sample validation and explicit uncertainty quantification
(the predictive variance $\sigma^2_z$ produced by each model)
are essential safeguards before deployment.

\textbf{Conclusions.}
We have proposed and implemented a framework for replacing the static IC with a
conditional, time-varying function $IC_t(z)$.
Five estimators — static, polynomial Kalman filter, RBF-RLS, binned Kalman,
and Nadaraya--Watson — are evaluated in a fully sequential walk-forward backtest
over 30 years of U.S.\ equity data.
If the conditional IC hypothesis holds, at least one dynamic model should achieve
a meaningfully higher Sharpe ratio than the static baseline across multiple signals.

Future directions include multi-signal IC estimation, uncertainty-adjusted MVO
(adding predictive variance to the diagonal of the covariance matrix),
and extending to non-U.S.\ equity universes.
