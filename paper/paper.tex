\title{Modeling the Dynamic Conditional Information Coefficient}
\author{Conner B. Draper, Seokkwon Yoon}

\date{\today}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
The Information Coefficient (IC) measures a signal's predictive power over realized returns and
is typically assumed constant at $IC \approx 0.05$.  We relax this by modeling a dynamic,
signal-strength-dependent function $IC_t(z)$, where $z$ is a cross-sectionally standardized
signal.  Five online-learning methods---a constant baseline, independent binned Kalman filters,
a polynomial Kalman filter, RBF recursive least squares, and Nadaraya-Watson kernel
regression---are evaluated across eight equity signals through walk-forward mean-variance
optimization over a 2010--2024 backtest period.  Dynamic models consistently outperform the
static baseline in risk-adjusted returns, confirming that predictive power concentrates in
signal extremes and shifts with market regime.
\end{abstract}

%% First Section
\section{Research Question and Overview of the Data}

Classical active management theory, formalized by Grinold and Kahn~\cite{GrinoldKahn1999},
states that the expected alpha for asset $i$ at time $t$ is
$\alpha_{i,t} = IC \cdot \sigma_{i,t} \cdot z_{i,t}$,
where $\sigma_{i,t}$ is idiosyncratic risk, $z_{i,t}$ is the standardized signal, and $IC$ is
treated as a universal constant.  Our central question is: \emph{does IC vary with signal
strength $z$ and with calendar time $t$, and can online learning models capture this variation
to improve portfolio performance?}

Prior work provides motivation but not resolution.  Clarke et al.~\cite{Clarke2002} document
regime-dependence of momentum ICs and Qian and Hua~\cite{QianHua2004} study time-varying IC in
factor timing; neither estimates the full conditional function $IC_t(z)$.  We fill this gap with
five online estimators applied to eight daily cross-sectional signals derived from a licensed
Barra U.S.\ equity factor model (2010--2025): \emph{style, industry, and idiosyncratic momentum
and reversal; idiosyncratic volatility; and betting-against-beta (BAB).}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Weaknesses.}  Daily return data is extremely noisy; realized Sharpe ratios are
    well below one.  Micro-cap stocks introduce large outliers and the factor model imposes
    a linear exposure structure that may misspecify tail behavior.
  \item \textbf{Suitability.}  Eight approximately uncorrelated signals satisfy the breadth
    requirement of the Fundamental Law, and Barra specific-risk estimates enable theoretically
    grounded risk normalization.
  \item \textbf{Expected findings.}  Tail $z$-scores ($|z| > 2$) will carry disproportionate
    predictive power, and this structure will vary across momentum-crash regimes.
  \item \textbf{Further questions.}  Can $IC_t(z)$ detect signal decay in real time?  Does the
    optimal function differ materially across sectors or market-cap strata?
\end{itemize}

%% Second Section
\section{Data Cleaning and Feature Engineering}

\textbf{Cross-sectional standardization.}  On each date $t$ the raw signal $s_{i,t}$ is
winsorized and standardized: $z_{i,t} = (s_{i,t} - \bar{s}_t) / \mathrm{std}_t(s)$, clipped to
$[-3, 3]$.  This ensures that all signals live on the same scale and prevents outliers from
dominating the regression.

\textbf{Risk-normalized target.}  Rather than regressing on raw returns $r_{i,t+1}$ we set
\[
  y_{i,t} = \frac{r_{i,t+1}}{\sigma_{i,t}},
\]
where $\sigma_{i,t}$ is the Barra specific risk.  Under the Grinold decomposition,
$\mathbb{E}[y_{i,t} \mid z_{i,t}] \approx IC_t(z_{i,t}) \cdot z_{i,t}$, so dividing by
$\sigma_{i,t}$ makes the regression intercept directly interpretable as the IC, removing the
confounding effect of heteroskedastic volatility.

\textbf{Universe filter.}  A minimum price filter ($> \$5.00$) removes illiquid micro-cap
stocks.  Retaining them would inflate apparent IC while introducing execution costs that
cannot be modeled in a daily rebalancing backtest.

\textbf{Walk-forward discipline.}  All model state is updated at time $t$ using only
$(z_{i,s}, y_{i,s})$ for $s \leq t$; portfolios are formed using forecasts for $t+1$.  This
eliminates look-ahead bias and mirrors a live trading environment.

%% Third Section
\section{Data Visualization and Basic Analysis}

By construction, each signal's cross-sectional $z$-distribution is approximately standard
normal.  The key non-trivial statistic is the \emph{realized IC}: the daily cross-sectional
correlation between $z_{i,t}$ and $y_{i,t+1}$.  Computed over a rolling 120-day window, the
realized IC for style momentum varies from $-0.04$ to $+0.12$, with sharp negative episodes
coinciding with documented momentum crashes (2020 COVID reversal, 2022 growth-to-value
rotation).  Binning $y_{i,t}$ by $z$-score decile reveals that the outer deciles contribute
two to three times more predictive power per unit of $z$ than the inner deciles, motivating
a non-constant $IC(z)$.

Figure~\ref{fig:IcCurves} visualizes this structure directly.  The left panel shows the
binned Kalman's learned piecewise IC; the right panel shows the polynomial Kalman at three
calendar checkpoints, illustrating regime change.  A flat $IC = 0.05$ line would miss both
the tail asymmetry and the temporal variation.

%%Fourth Section
\section{Learning Algorithms and In-Depth Analysis}

Each model produces a forecast $\hat{\alpha}_{i,t} = \widehat{IC_t}(z_{i,t}) \cdot z_{i,t}$
which is multiplied by $\sigma_{i,t}$ to recover the alpha in return units and fed into the
optimizer.

\subsection{Static Baseline}
$\widehat{IC_t}(z) = 0.05$ for all $z, t$, giving $\hat{\alpha}_{i,t} = 0.05\,\sigma_{i,t}\,z_{i,t}$.

\subsection{Binned Kalman Filters}
The $z$-axis is partitioned into $N = 15$ equal-width bins $\mathcal{B}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{B}_N$.
Each bin $k$ runs an independent scalar Kalman filter tracking $IC_k^t$.  For assets in bin $k$,
the observation $y_i / z_i \approx IC_k$ holds when $|z_i| \geq 0.2$; the within-bin mean
$\hat{IC}_{k,t} = n_{k,t}^{-1}\!\sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}_k} y_{i,t}/z_{i,t}$
has effective variance $R/n_{k,t}$.  The filter recursion is
\begin{align*}
  p_{k,t}^{-} &= p_{k,t-1} + Q, \qquad
  K_{k,t} = \frac{p_{k,t}^{-}}{p_{k,t}^{-} + R/n_{k,t}}, \\
  IC_{k,t} &= IC_{k,t-1} + K_{k,t}\!\left(\hat{IC}_{k,t} - IC_{k,t-1}\right), \qquad
  p_{k,t} = (1 - K_{k,t})\,p_{k,t}^{-},
\end{align*}
with $Q = 10^{-4}$, $R = 1$, and prior $IC_{k,0} = 0.05$.

\subsection{Polynomial Kalman Filter}
We parameterize $IC_t(z) = \beta_{0,t} + \beta_{1,t} z + \beta_{2,t} z^2$, so
$\hat\alpha_z = IC_t(z)\cdot z = \beta_{0,t} z + \beta_{1,t} z^2 + \beta_{2,t} z^3$.
Defining $\boldsymbol\beta_t = [\beta_0,\beta_1,\beta_2]^\top$ and the per-asset observation
row $\mathbf{h}_i = [z_i,\, z_i^2,\, z_i^3]$, the batch observation model for the full
cross-section is $\mathbf{y}_t = H_t\boldsymbol\beta_t + \boldsymbol\varepsilon_t$ with
$\boldsymbol\varepsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, R\,I)$.  The state evolves as a random
walk $\boldsymbol\beta_t = \boldsymbol\beta_{t-1} + \mathbf{w}_t$, $\mathbf{w}_t \sim
\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, Q\,I)$~\cite{Kalman1960}.  The Kalman update is:
\begin{align*}
  P_t^{-} &= P_{t-1} + Q\,I, \qquad
  S_t = H_t P_t^{-} H_t^\top + R\,I_n, \qquad
  K_t = P_t^{-} H_t^\top S_t^{-1}, \\
  \boldsymbol\beta_t &= \boldsymbol\beta_t^{-} + K_t\!\left(\mathbf{y}_t - H_t\boldsymbol\beta_t^{-}\right),
  \qquad P_t = (I - K_t H_t)\,P_t^{-}.
\end{align*}
The initial state $\boldsymbol\beta_0 = [0, 0.05, 0]^\top$ matches the static baseline.

\subsection{RBF Recursive Least Squares}
We fix $K = 7$ Gaussian basis functions centered at $c_k \in \{-3,-2,\ldots,3\}$:
$\varphi_k(z) = \exp\!\bigl(-(z-c_k)^2/2\sigma^2\bigr)$, and model
$\hat\alpha_z = \boldsymbol\Phi\,\mathbf{w}$.
Applying the matrix-inversion lemma to the weighted least-squares criterion with forgetting
factor $\lambda = 0.995$ yields the RLS update~\cite{Haykin2002}:
\begin{align*}
  A_t &= \lambda I_n + \Phi_t P_{t-1} \Phi_t^\top, \qquad
  K_t = P_{t-1}\Phi_t^\top A_t^{-1}, \\
  \mathbf{w}_t &= \mathbf{w}_{t-1} + K_t(\mathbf{y}_t - \Phi_t \mathbf{w}_{t-1}), \qquad
  P_t = \lambda^{-1}(P_{t-1} - K_t \Phi_t P_{t-1}).
\end{align*}
The forgetting factor down-weights observations aged $\Delta t$ days by $\lambda^{\Delta t}$,
giving an effective half-life of $\log 2 / |\!\log\lambda| \approx 138$ days.

\subsection{Nadaraya-Watson Kernel Regression}
The Nadaraya-Watson estimator~\cite{Nadaraya1964,Watson1964} defines the conditional mean as:
\[
  \hat{f}_t(z) = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{s \leq t}\sum_i K_s(z, z_{i,s})\,\kappa_t(t-s)\,y_{i,s}}
                      {\displaystyle\sum_{s \leq t}\sum_i K_s(z, z_{i,s})\,\kappa_t(t-s)},
\]
where $K_s(z,z') = \exp\!\bigl(-(z-z')^2/2h_s^2\bigr)$ is a Gaussian spatial kernel
($h_s = 0.5$) and $\kappa_t(\Delta t) = e^{-\Delta t/\tau}$ is exponential temporal decay
($\tau = 60$ days).  Storing the full history would require $O(T \cdot n)$ memory and
$O(T \cdot n^2)$ work per day.  Instead we maintain running sums
$S_w(z_g) = \sum k_s k_t$ and $S_{wy}(z_g) = \sum k_s k_t y$ on a grid of 200 points
aged by $e^{-1/\tau}$ each day, reducing per-day cost to $O(n_{\text{grid}} \cdot n_{\text{obs}})$.

\subsection{Mean-Variance Optimization}

Given alpha forecasts $\mu_{i,t} = \sigma_{i,t}\,\hat\alpha_{i,t}$ and the Barra factor
covariance matrix $\Sigma_t$, we solve
\[
  \max_w \;\; w^\top \mu_t - \tfrac{\gamma}{2}\,w^\top \Sigma_t w
  \quad \text{s.t.} \quad
  w^\top \boldsymbol\beta_{\mathrm{mkt}} = 0, \quad \mathbf{1}^\top w = 0,
\]
with risk aversion $\gamma = 200$.  The constraints enforce market-neutrality (zero-beta) and
dollar-neutrality (zero-investment).  Forming the Lagrangian
$\mathcal{L} = w^\top\mu - \frac{\gamma}{2}w^\top\Sigma w - \lambda_1 w^\top\boldsymbol\beta - \lambda_2 \mathbf{1}^\top w$
and applying the KKT condition $\nabla_w \mathcal{L} = 0$ gives
\[
  w^* = \frac{1}{\gamma}\,\Sigma_t^{-1}\!\left(\mu_t - \lambda_1^*\boldsymbol\beta_{\mathrm{mkt}} - \lambda_2^*\mathbf{1}\right),
\]
showing the optimal portfolio is the unconstrained maximum-Sharpe allocation $\Sigma_t^{-1}\mu_t$
projected onto the feasible set.  Multipliers $\lambda_1^*, \lambda_2^*$ are solved analytically
via a $2\times2$ linear system.  In practice the optimization is handled by CVXPY.

Walk-forward results over 2010--2024 confirm that dynamic models---particularly the polynomial
Kalman and binned Kalman---generate higher Sharpe ratios than the static baseline across the
majority of signal-model pairs, validating the hypothesis that $IC_t(z)$ is non-trivially
non-constant.

%%Fifth Section
\section{Ethical Implications and Conclusions}

Three ethical concerns arise from deploying dynamic IC models at scale.

\textbf{Crowding and self-defeating feedback.}  If many institutions simultaneously estimate
$IC_t(z)$ and tilt toward high-$|z|$ assets, correlated demand compresses or reverses the
very premium being exploited.  The zero-beta and zero-investment constraints limit single-factor
concentration but do not eliminate cross-model crowding.  Position limits and turnover controls
are necessary complements.

\textbf{Access inequality.}  Constructing these models requires licensed Barra factor
exposures, daily-frequency data, and significant compute---resources unavailable to retail
investors.  Widespread institutional adoption could widen the information gap between
professional and retail participants.

\textbf{Model fragility.}  All five estimators assume a degree of stationarity in the
$IC_t(z)$ relationship.  Structural breaks (post-2020 rate-regime change, 2022 growth-to-value
rotation) can cause rapid parameter mis-calibration, potentially generating large drawdowns
when risk is highest.  Ongoing monitoring for IC collapse and automatic de-risking rules
are essential safeguards.

In conclusion, we demonstrate that the conditional IC $IC_t(z)$ is non-constant in both $z$
and $t$.  Online Kalman and kernel learning methods successfully estimate this structure in a
walk-forward setting, yielding meaningful improvements in risk-adjusted portfolio performance
relative to the static Grinold baseline.
